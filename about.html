Why I Made This Website

  I was first introduced to nonograms through Pokémon Picross in early 2016. I played the game infrequently and one day forgot about it entirely. In the spring of 2024, I was reminded of the game and decided to try it out again. I quickly noticed a connection between nonograms and combinatorics, an area of mathematics I was briefly introduced to at the time. I began formulating a question I could write some code to answer, and I finished writing my first python program in August of 2024 counting how many n x n grids are possible while increasing the number of colors on the grid. I discovered from this that ignoring the concept of clues and focusing on the individual cells of nonograms with this idea of color is a way to represent the Worpitzky numbers.

  I took a break for a few months to work on other projects and decided to create some puzzles so I have the freedom to play from my phone, and can easily introduce others to the concept. This website is the result of that.

  The original question I set out to answer remains unknown to me, but I plan to study popular solving algorithms and answer it with a future project.

Future Nonogram Projects

  Nonograms are a fantastic application for computer science and mathematics alike. I plan on expanding on my previous project to establish how many colors need to exist on a grid for the grid to have only unique solutions. I also hope to prove that grids that appear symmetric but aren't symmetric cannot have a unique solution, as these parameters always introduce a switching component to the grid.

  Other modifications of nonograms with potential for research questions are triddlers – a nonogram game with an additional dimension on a hexagon shaped grid, Mega picross – a standard grid where 2 neighboring rows of clues can be merged to form mega clues, and murals – a cluster of several nonogram games that comprised create one big image.
